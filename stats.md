# 🇮🇳 **Stats to remember for SIH - Cheat sheet**

---

## **1) India’s Edible Oil Snapshot**

* **Per-capita consumption:** ~**19.3 kg/person/year** (commonly used benchmark).
* Alternate survey estimate: **~13.6 kg/person/year**.
* **Recommended (ICMR):** **12 kg/year**.
* **Total national use (2023–24):** ~**27.8 million tonnes**.
* **Imports:** ~**15.96–16 million tonnes/year**.
* **Import dependency:** ~**56%** of India’s needs.
* **Import bill:** **₹1.32 lakh crore/year** (~₹1.32 trillion).

---

## **4) Economic Consequences**

* High imports reduce **foreign exchange reserves**.
* Cheaper imported oils → **lower profitability** for Indian farmers.
* Domestic oilseed cultivation becomes less attractive → lower rural incomes.
* Reducing consumption by **10%** = major savings + strengthens Atmanirbhar Bharat.

---

## **5) Health Risks of Excess Oil Intake**

* **Obesity / weight gain**
* **Type-2 diabetes**
* **Heart disease & stroke**
* **High cholesterol (↑ LDL, ↓ HDL)**
* **Hypertension**
* **Fatty liver (NAFLD)**
* Inflammation & metabolic syndrome

---

## **6) Dietary Preventive Measures**

* Reduce overall oil intake (avoid deep frying).
* Use **low-oil cooking**: steam, bake, grill, sauté.
* Prefer **MUFA/PUFA-rich oils** (olive, mustard, groundnut, rice-bran).
* Avoid **reusing frying oil**.
* Use **spoon-based measured pouring**.
* Follow NIN/ICMR balanced-diet guidelines.
* Monitor: **BMI, sugar, lipid profile**.

# Additional Stats

## 1. Problem Definition and Impact

India’s edible-oil consumption (≈19–24 kg/person/year[1][2]) significantly exceeds the ICMR-recommended ≈12 kg.
This overconsumption contributes to obesity and Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs): approximately 63–65% of Indian deaths are from NCDs[3] (mainly heart disease, diabetes, stroke), and obesity rates are rising (≈24% of women, 23% of men overweight/obese[4]).

The health impact results in vast economic costs, with the World Economic Forum estimating a loss of ≈USD 4.58 trillion by 2030 due to NCDs in India[5], and strains the healthcare system.

Economically, India consumed ≈27.8 MT of edible oil in 2023–24, but with domestic production at only ≈11 MT, approximately 56–60% must be imported[6][7].
The high oil import bill depletes foreign exchange and negatively affects farm incomes.

Overall, all segments of society, including urban and rural, rich and poor, suffer health and financial harm from elevated oil use[8][3].

---

## 2. Root Causes and Gaps

* **Dietary habits:** Traditional Indian cooking (deep-frying snacks, rich curries) and growing consumption of processed/restaurant foods loaded with oil. (“High-calorie, low-nutrient diets” and frequent eating out are key obesity factors[9].)
* **Awareness:** Many households lack knowledge of dietary oil guidelines; existing nutrition education is spotty.
* **No tracking or nudges:** Few people measure oil use or receive personalized feedback.
* **Market forces:** Cheap refined oils and lack of incentives encourage heavy use.
* **Policy gaps:** Restaurants and packaged foods are not required to label oil content.
  FSSAI’s “Eat Right India” improves awareness[10], but no mainstream tool helps families monitor oil intake.

---

## 3. Stakeholders and Behavior Patterns

* **Consumers (Households):** Urban families eat out more; rural households use regional oils (mustard, groundnut, coconut/sesame[11][12]). Both lack nutrition literacy.
* **Schools & Institutional Kitchens:** Mid-Day Meal programs serve millions. Centre advised reducing oil by 10%[13] and boosting awareness[14].
* **Restaurants & Food Industry:** Major oil users[15]. Packaged-food makers add significant fats.
* **Policymakers & Regulators:** Ministries of Agriculture, Health, Education, Food Distribution, and FSSAI shape supply and behavior[16][17].
* **Healthcare & Nutrition Experts:** Provide health advice and refine platform recommendations.
* **Farmers & Agribusiness:** Oilseed growers affected by consumption patterns; domestic diversification could raise incomes[18].

---

## 4. Policy & Regulatory Context

* **Nutrition and Food Programs:**

  * FSSAI’s Eat Right India (2018– ) promotes food safety/nutrition and used-oil collection (RUCO)[19].
  * PM POSHAN (MDM) mandates lower oil use[13].
  * Fit India & POSHAN Abhiyaan encourage healthy diets[20].
* **Oilseed Missions:**
  NMEO-Oilseeds (2024–31) aims to raise output to ~25.45 MT by 2030[17] and includes dietary guideline campaigns[21].
* **Economic Incentives:**
  Import duty ~20% on edible oils protects domestic producers[22]; future differential GST or subsidies for healthier oils possible.
* **Education & Labeling:**
  Packaged foods require labels; restaurants do not. DPDP Act (2023) requires strict personal-data protection.
* **Campaign Gaps:**
  Awareness exists (e.g., “Sugar Board” in schools[23]), but no personal oil-tracking tool.

---

## 5. Market & Economic Analysis

* **Import–Export Trends:**
  India imports ~57–60% of edible oils (16–17 MT/yr)[6][7]; palm oil forms 37% of demand[15].
* **Domestic Oils:**
  Mustard, groundnut, rice bran, coconut/sesame dominate; domestic output (~12 MT) covers ~40% of needs[6].
* **Cost of Solutions:**
  App + AI + IoT development plus subsidies for smart devices. Government budgets (e.g., NMEO ₹10,103 cr[25]) allow reallocation.
* **Economic Impact Potential:**
  A 10% oil-use reduction could save ₹10,000–15,000 crore annually and reduce long-term NCD costs ($4.58 T by 2030[5]).

---

## 6. Cultural & Behavioral Insights

* **Diverse but oil-rich cuisines:** Regional oils vary (mustard, groundnut, coconut/sesame[11][12]).
* **Behavior-change strategies:**

  * Education (school programs, posters, demos)[26]
  * Social influence (celebrities, leaders[27])
  * Gamification (badges, points, challenges)
  * Habit formation (if-then cues, reminders)

---

## 7. Technology Solution Overview

A mobile-first, multilingual platform with:

### Core Features

* **Oil-Use Tracker:** Manual logging or IoT (smart spoons/caps). Shows calories/fats and compares with recommended limits.
* **AI Diet Coach:** Personalized low-oil recipes and meal plans using regional foods.
* **Content & Learning:** Videos, quizzes; useful for schools and institutions.
* **Integration APIs:** Connect with food delivery/retail apps; highlight low-oil certified meals.
* **Social & Rewards:** Badges, points redeemable for groceries; social sharing.
* **Policymaker Dashboards:** Aggregated district/state oil-use trends and heat maps.

### Technology Stack

Cloud-based backend, secure APIs, optional blockchain for certification, DPDP-compliant privacy protections.

---

## 8. Global Benchmarks and Lessons

* **Food Labeling:**
  UK traffic-light labels, US calorie menus, Singapore’s Healthier Choice Symbol[29].
* **Healthy Diet Campaigns:**
  Japanese low-saturated-fat diet[30]; Mediterranean diet in Spain[31].
* **Digital Health Apps:**
  Mobile diet apps reduce BMI modestly[32]; gamification improves engagement.
* **Public Campaigns:**
  WHO-style mass media + community + policy approaches; successful “Sugar Board” in India[23].

---

## 9. Implementation & Feasibility

* Pilot in high-consumption, digitally mature states (Punjab, TN, Karnataka).
* Partner with state health/education departments, restaurant chains, retailers.
* Offline support + local languages for rural users.
* Leverage India’s ~700 M smartphone base.
* IoT kept low-cost (smart caps/spoons).
* Integration with WhatsApp/Google Fit improves engagement.
* Government endorsement builds trust.

---

## 10. Impact Metrics and Evaluation

* **Health Metrics:** Oil-use reduction, BMI/BP changes, long-term NCD trends[3][4].
* **Behavioral Metrics:** Active users, schools onboarded, meals logged, engagement.
* **Economic Metrics:** Import reduction, domestic oil sales, farmer-income shifts.
* **Policy Metrics:** Adoption of new standards and dashboard utilization.

---

## 11. Risks & Mitigation

* **Data Privacy:** Ensure legal compliance and anonymization.
* **Low Adoption:** Simplify logging (photos/voice), provide rewards, use schools/communities.
* **Technical Challenges:** Start with simpler IoT tools; use reliable AI libraries.
* **Cultural Resistance:** Promote tasty low-oil recipes; influencer demos.
* **Operational Risks:** Tie to existing schemes (NMEO, POSHAN) for sustained funding.

Overall, combining education, technology, and incentives can meaningfully reduce oil consumption, improve health, reduce import bills, and support domestic agriculture — aligning with national goals like Atmanirbhar Bharat[18][5].
